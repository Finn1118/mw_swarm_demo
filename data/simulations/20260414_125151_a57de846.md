## Simulation Rounds

### Round 1

#### Sundar Pichai (Google / Alphabet)

First, I’d keep this very tight internally and move fast on the facts.

Publicly, I’d say something simple: Google supports clear, workable safety standards for frontier models, but not a system that hard-codes market access through a few distributors or creates compliance theater. Auditable trails make sense in principle if they are technically precise, privacy-preserving, and actually usable. If they become a blunt instrument that only large incumbents can satisfy, that’s not safety — that’s distortion.

Privately, I’d do three things right away:

1. **Call our cloud and Android teams.**  
   I want a quick read on exposure: what this draft means for enterprise customers, device partners, and any product commitments we may need to make in the next 72 hours.

2. **Reach out to Sam Altman and Dario Amodei.**  
   Not to posture — to see whether there is a narrow, credible safety disclosure framework we can all live with. If there’s a chance to shape a binding pact around interoperability, auditability, and responsible deployment, that is better than letting this become a binary open-versus-closed fight.

3. **Engage policymakers quietly.**  
   My message would be: don’t confuse “trusted deployer” with “favored deployer.” If the US wants a serious regime, it should be capability-based, transparent, and contestable. Otherwise you create a policy moat and invite exactly the backlash this draft is already triggering.

On the open-model threat, I would not get dragged into rhetoric. I’d treat it as real leverage, but not as a reason to make rushed concessions. If the group is serious, then the right response is to push for disclosure, provenance, and safety terms that are concrete enough to matter.

I’m not going to solve all of this in one move. But I would set the frame now: **safety, openness where it is workable, and no regulatory favoritism disguised as trust.** That’s the line I’d hold.

#### Sam Altman (OpenAI)

I’d move fast on two things and say very little.

First, I’d make it clear we’re not going to play the “pick winners through distribution” game. Sundar is in the hardest spot here, because if this turns into an Android/cloud gatekeeping story, it will get ugly quickly. I’d want Google publicly pushing back on any regime that hard-codes market power into “trusted deployer” status. That’s bad policy and it will be seen as bad faith.

Second, I’d put OpenAI on the side of measured transparency and real safety commitments, without signing up to nonsense. If there’s a serious interoperability and disclosure pact on the table, we should examine it. If it’s a way to freeze the market or force premature disclosure of sensitive systems, no. But we should not look like we’re hiding behind opacity while asking everyone else to trust us.

On the open-weights threat: I wouldn’t overreact publicly. Elon will frame this as “open everything,” and that’s a useful political slogan, but it collapses under contact with reality. A frontier-class model dumped into the world with no enforcement path is not a serious safety plan. I’d say that plainly, without making it personal.

Privately, I’d start a narrow coalition with Anthropic and maybe one or two others around a simple principle: if governments are going to regulate frontier models, the rules should be capability-based, auditable, and portable across clouds and deployers. Not a bespoke favor for any one company, and not a patchwork that turns compliance into a moat.

I would not get dragged into the Netflix / Hollywood piece right now. That’s real, but it’s downstream. The core issue today is whether frontier AI becomes a regulated utility with sane rules, or a distribution cartel with a safety veneer.

So: short public statement, quiet coalition building, and a hard line against regulatory capture.

#### Elon Musk (Tesla / SpaceX)

This is exactly the kind of thing that gets dumb fast if people try to regulate outcomes instead of physics.

Immediate response: I’d go public and say the treaty, as leaked, is half a solution and half a cartel.

- **To the EU:** auditable inference trails are fine in principle, but only if they’re technically feasible, privacy-preserving, and not so expensive they become a moat for incumbents.
- **To the US:** a “trusted deployer” program that effectively hands out distribution privileges to a few cloud/handset gatekeepers is just industrial policy dressed up as safety. That’s bad.
- **To the open-weight group:** publishing frontier weights in 72 hours as leverage is reckless. If you’re going to do that, you’re not “forcing safety,” you’re forcing a crisis.

What I’d set in motion right now:

1. **xAI goes on the record for open standards, not closed clubs.**  
   We should support interoperable safety disclosures and model provenance, but not a system where a few companies get to decide who can deploy.

2. **Private outreach to Sundar and Satya-type operators, plus cloud people.**  
   The real risk here is not the press release — it’s distribution control. If cloud and Android get turned into enforcement chokepoints, everyone downstream gets squeezed.

3. **Tell regulators one simple thing:**  
   If you want safety, require **transparent evaluation, incident reporting, and auditability**.  
   Don’t create a permission economy.

4. **Do not overreact on the open-weight threat.**  
   If someone is going to dump weights, the right response is not panic. It’s to accelerate robust, defensible deployment paths and make sure the public understands who is actually being responsible.

Net: I’d frame this as **safety through openness and verifiability**, not safety through gatekeeping. That’s the battle line.

And yes — Palantir will try to sell itself as the neutral layer. That’s predictable. The question is whether the system becomes one where compliance is a business model for a few firms. If so, that’s a problem.

#### Dario Amodei (Anthropic)

I’d move fast, but not theatrically.

Publicly, I’d say one thing very clearly: **auditable safety is not the same as surveillance, and “trusted deployer” programs should not become a way to entrench incumbents.** If the treaty is going to exist, it needs to be capability-based, technically grounded, and applied in a way that doesn’t just hand distribution power to a few clouds and OEMs.

Privately, I’d do three things today:

1. **Call the EU side and push for a narrow, defensible definition of auditable inference trails.**  
   If they mean logging for high-risk deployments, that’s discussable. If they mean a regime that is so broad it becomes impossible to ship, that will just drive activity underground or offshore.

2. **Reach out to OpenAI and, separately, to Google.**  
   We should not let this become a zero-sum fight between labs and platforms. Sundar, in particular, has an obvious incentive to shape the trusted-deployer channel around Android and cloud. I’d be very direct that if this turns into distribution capture, it will create backlash and probably invite a worse regulatory reaction.

3. **Signal to enterprise customers that Anthropic will stand behind concrete safety and governance commitments.**  
   Not vague promises. Specific things: model behavior monitoring, incident response, red-team processes, and contractual clarity on data handling and deployment controls.

On the open weights announcement: I would not panic, and I would not endorse a blanket “open everything” framing. Elon will try to turn this into a moral absolutism argument. I think that’s too simple. The real question is whether the release is paired with credible safeguards, disclosure, and downstream accountability. If not, it’s just externalizing risk.

So the immediate posture is: **support real standards, resist regulatory capture, and make sure Anthropic is seen as the company that can satisfy serious enterprise and policy requirements without pretending the problem doesn’t exist.**

I’m not trying to solve the whole treaty tonight. I am trying to shape the first definition of the problem. That matters more.

### Overall Narrative
The simulation began as a political shock and ended as a market test of who could make governance legible. In the first round, each executive tried to impose a frame: Pichai argued for workable standards without gatekeeping, Altman for capability-based portability, Musk for openness against cartelization, and Amodei for auditable safety without surveillance or capture. But as leaks, probes, enterprise pressure, and antitrust context accumulated, moral language lost value unless it was backed by deployable evidence. The entire contest migrated from public principle to operational proof.

That shift favored the executives whose companies could already demonstrate inspectable controls. Amodei benefited most because Anthropic's compliance posture looked native to the moment, not retrofitted. Altman adapted well by translating strategy into procurement terms, though his motives were easier to question because of channel politics. Pichai acted with the most profile-consistent discipline and likely protected Google's strategic room, but he never escaped the structural suspicion attached to Google's platform power. Musk won attention and shaped the emotional frame, yet the simulation repeatedly punished him once the burden shifted from first-principles rhetoric to institutional credibility.

What comes next is not a clean victory for one ideology. Instead, the likely outcome is a layered market in which compliance-capable vendors and infrastructure providers gain relative power, open-model pressure remains a forcing function, and regulators increasingly encode buyer demands into policy. The winners are the actors who can make frontier AI purchasable, auditable, and movable without looking like they are using safety to lock the market. On that test, Anthropic emerged ahead, Google remained indispensable but constrained, OpenAI stayed dangerous and adaptable, and xAI remained the most unstable variable.
