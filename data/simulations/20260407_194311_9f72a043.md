### Overall Narrative
The simulation began as a familiar AI power contest and ended as a structural repricing of what power actually means. At first, each executive tried to defend his core franchise: Pichai through calm coordination and platform breadth, Karp through control and strategic aggression, Amodei through safety-backed credibility, and Altman through scale and deployment strength. But the external shocks changed the rules. Once regulators focused on concentration and customers began demanding supply-chain guarantees, fallback rights, and optionality, the market stopped rewarding the loudest claims of frontier leadership and started rewarding architectures that could absorb volatility.

That shift advantaged the executives who could redefine their companies as layers rather than singular products. Pichai was best positioned to do this because Google could monetize ecosystem fragmentation through cloud, security, and partner distribution. Karp saw fastest that fragmentation creates a control-plane opportunity, though his success risks turning Palantir into a new target. Amodei benefited because Anthropic's trust language was backed by real enterprise traction and multi-cloud resilience, but his company now has to prove that becoming central does not erode its safety identity. Altman remained formidable, yet he was the one most visibly pushed off his preferred terrain; he spent the simulation trying to stop optionality from becoming the market's default grammar.

What comes next is less about who has the best model than who can define the safest and most governable way to buy, route, and replace models. In that world, infrastructure owners, compliance layers, and trusted second-source providers gain leverage. The likely future is not one champion crushing the field, but a stack in which power is redistributed upward to cloud and orchestration, sideways to open-model alternatives, and downward to procurement teams that now know they can force architectural concessions.
